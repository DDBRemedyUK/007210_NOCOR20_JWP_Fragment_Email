Following our recent discussion around Joint Working Projects (JWPs), please find below some further details that you may find interesting.

We have also included a short case study that you may find useful and relevant, but please contact us if you require further information.

JWPs are non-promotional arrangements between NHS organisations and Novartis, where, for the benefit of patients, Novartis (either solely or jointly with another pharmaceutical company) and the NHS pool skills, experience and resources for the joint development and implementation of a patient-centred project.1


JWPs can help the NHS improve cancer services
and patient-reported outcomes through:

The Hammersmith Hospital Myeloproliferative Neoplasm (MPN) Service Database Joint Working Project
Novartis partnered with the Hammersmith clinical haematology team to identify improvements in the clinical pathway for patients with MPN.

Between 2018 and 2019, patients reported an increase in awareness of services, such as holistic care and telephone consultations, with new clinic appointment templates in place to minimise waiting times.

The North Central London Sickle and Thalassaemia Network Service Development Joint Working Project
Following an internal peer review by the Trust management team in 2016, Novartis undertook a JWP with the University College London Hospital (UCLH) and Whittington Hospital Network to improve and standardise activities and functions, including the operation of clinics and the regularity and quality of educational meetings for staff and patients.

As a result of the Network improvements, there was a reduction in the UCLH ‘Did not attend’ haemoglobinopathy outpatient clinic rate, indicating improved patient engagement and care.

Nottingham University Hospital: Joint Working Partnership with Novartis in melanoma and renal cell carcinoma
In a 2-year JWP between Novartis and The Nottingham University Hospital, the role of an additional melanoma and renal cancer Clinical Nurse Specialist was assessed to see how it impacted patient care.

After 2 years, attendance at MDT meetings, holistic needs assessments and patient experiences all improved. As well as evidencing benefits to the patients and HCPs, the Trust was able to evidence a financially viable service.

University College London Hospital
(UCLH) Cancer Collaborative: Novartis
Joint Working Agreement
In this JWP with Novartis, the ULCH aimed to improve information available to multidisciplinary teams (MDTs) about their patients and increase engagement with cancer data.

Engagement from clinical teams varied, with those who were engaged seeing the biggest improvement in data quality. Increasing engagement with cancer data can be used by clinical teams to reduce variations and provide enhanced patient pathways and tailored care.

Joint Working Projects between The Christie Hospital, Greater Manchester, and Novartis to identify and address inequalities in breast cancer care
Novartis and The Christie NHS Foundation Trust worked together on two JWPs to understand and address inequity of access to the secondary breast cancer service (sBC) across Greater Manchester.

The first JWP used multiple data sources to identify areas of disparity and where the most patient burden could be saved with easier access to therapy.

Based on these insights, the second JWP part-funded a new care model that worked towards earlier identification of patients, with two pharmacy clinics and a telephone clinic to help improve patient experience.

After 1 year:
•   There was an increase in the number of patients consenting to clinical trials
•   The Christie was able to offer an expanded service
•   There was an overall positive trend in patient satisfaction

The Joint Working Partnership
and Industry Engagement with
the Scottish Genetics Laboratories
In a partnership between NHS GG&C Laboratory and Genetics, Novartis and Celgene, this JWP looked to develop the diagnostic, prognostic and therapeutic pathways in cancer care.

With the introduction of a next-generation sequencing panel, 28 new targets for AML genes could be identified. This offered more accurate diagnosis, tailored care, and greater inclusion in clinical trials and potential future targets. This implementation paved the way for other tumour-specific tests now in service, including a test for multiple cancer types.
